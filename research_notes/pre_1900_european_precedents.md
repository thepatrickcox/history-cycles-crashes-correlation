# Research Note: Pre-1900 Precedents — Do the Patterns Predate the United States?

**Status: Exploratory. Not a finding. Hypothesis and research agenda only.**
*Patrick Cox — June 2026*

---

## Why this note exists

The most common objection to this project is sample size: roughly fourteen major US market events over ~115 years is a small number to draw structural conclusions from.

There is a direct answer to that objection (see `METHODOLOGY.md` — "On sample size and the complete-record problem"): the US events are not a sample drawn from a larger pool. They are the complete population. The US has only existed since 1776 and has only had meaningful equity markets since the late nineteenth century. You cannot collect more US crashes than have occurred any more than a geologist can collect more than one Earth.

But there is a *stronger* answer, and it is testable: **if the same patterns — market crashes clustering with war, political stress, and wealth concentration — appear in older states that existed before the US, then the patterns are not artifacts of American institutions. They are general features of how markets, war finance, and political power interact.**

This note lays out the hypothesis and the candidate evidence. None of it is confirmed. All of it requires primary-source research before any of it enters `FINDINGS.md`.

---

## The hypothesis

**H-PRE:** The structural relationships observed in the US dataset (crash clustering with political stress; war finance as a wealth-concentration mechanism; restriction-then-adoption cycles in stores of value; synchronization across coupled markets) are observable in European financial history before 1900, and in non-Western imperial finance before that — suggesting the patterns are properties of organized economies under stress rather than properties of the modern United States specifically.

If true, this dramatically widens the evidentiary base and removes the sample-size objection. If false — if the pre-US record looks structurally different — that is equally informative and would suggest the US patterns are institution-specific.

## On war finance and wealth concentration (the defensible Rothschild point)

The Rothschild family's rise around the Napoleonic Wars is documented economic history and belongs in the **defensible** tier, *provided it is framed correctly.*

**What is sourced and defensible:**
- The Rothschild banking houses financed governments during the Napoleonic Wars and its aftermath, and accumulated substantial wealth through sovereign war finance and bond markets.
- Nathan Mayer Rothschild's London house profited significantly from British government finance in the 1815–1820s period.
- War finance generally — lending to states at war, trading the resulting sovereign debt — has historically been among the largest wealth-concentration mechanisms available, because wars require enormous capital quickly and states will pay heavily for it.
- This is Piketty-adjacent (*Capital in the Twenty-First Century*) and consistent with the established literature on sovereign debt and war.

**What is NOT sourced and must stay out of the defensible tier:**
- The widely circulated quotation attributed to Nathan Rothschild — paraphrased as caring nothing for which ruler sits on the throne so long as he controls the money supply — is **of unverified provenance.** It appears in many popular sources but has no established primary documentation. It must not be presented as a real quote.
- The dramatized "Waterloo fake-news bond coup" story (that Rothschild used early news of Waterloo to manipulate and corner the bond market) is **largely myth**, debunked by historians. The actual profits came from war finance over years, not a single dramatic trade.
- Any claim of deliberate orchestration of wars or crashes by the family is speculation and belongs in the private speculative archive, not here.

**The defensible framing for the dataset:** *War finance has historically concentrated wealth in the hands of those positioned to fund states at war. The Rothschild houses are the best-documented European example. This is a structural observation about war and capital, not a claim about orchestration.*

## Candidate events — Non-Western imperial finance (exploratory, lower confidence)

This is genuinely uncharted territory for the project and the sourcing is harder. Listed as open questions, not claims.

| Subject | Why it's worth investigating | Major caveat |
|---|---|---|
| **Song Dynasty China (960–1279)** | World's first government paper money (jiaozi); documented inflation episodes when over-issued; arguably the first fiat-currency boom-bust cycles in history | Sources are sparse and require sinological expertise; "crash" is not directly comparable to an equity market |
| **Yuan Dynasty (1271–1368)** | Mongol-ruled China; paper money hyperinflation contributed to dynastic collapse | Same sourcing difficulty; causation debated |
| **Ming Dynasty silver** | China's absorption of New World silver (16th–17th c.) tied Chinese monetary stability to global silver flows — a genuine cross-continental monetary linkage | Connects to the silver thread in F-03, but indirectly |
| **Mongol Empire / Genghis Khan finance** | The wars are well known; the *finance* of the conquests is far less studied — how campaigns were funded, how plunder was distributed, whether there were monetary consequences | This is a real research gap, but it may be a gap because the records largely don't exist. War finance in a pre-monetary-market empire may not map onto the framework at all. |
| **Roman currency debasement** | The progressive debasement of the denarius (1st–3rd c. CE) is well-documented and tied to military spending and political instability — arguably the clearest ancient parallel to the debt/debasement findings (F-07) | Not a market crash; a slow monetary-erosion pattern |

**Honest assessment of the non-Western material:** The Chinese paper-money episodes (Song/Yuan) and Roman debasement are the most promising because they are *monetary* phenomena with documented boom-bust or erosion dynamics. The Mongol/Genghis Khan angle is fascinating but may not yield usable financial data — the wars are documented, the *finance* may not be. Worth a focused look specifically because you noted you've never researched it; that's exactly the kind of unexamined corner where something might be hiding. But go in expecting the records may not support a rigorous comparison.

## What would make this publishable

1. **Source each European event** to established financial-history references (Kindleberger's *Manias, Panics, and Crashes* covers most of them; Reinhart-Rogoff's dataset extends back centuries and would be the single best source).
2. **Test the 1720 pair and 1873** against the synchronization gradient (F-06a). If markets synchronized across borders in eras with no telegraph, no shared regulator, and completely different institutions, that strengthens the gradient finding considerably.
3. **Keep the war-finance / wealth-concentration thread strictly in documented-history framing.** It is strong as economic history and fatal as conspiracy. The line is whether you claim concentration *happened* (defensible) or was *orchestrated* (speculative).
4. **Treat the non-Western material as a separate, clearly-lower-confidence appendix.** Don't let the weakest-sourced material undermine the well-sourced European events.
