# Investigation Column — Oil Supply Shocks as a Crash Precondition

**Status: CANDIDATE finding, under construction. Not yet graded. This document sets up the test; it does not claim the result.**

## The claim, stated as a sequence (not a correlation)

The hypothesis is an *ordering* claim, which makes it more falsifiable than a correlation, not less:

> A deliberate or conflict-driven **removal of oil supply from the world market** tends to appear **before** a major US market decline — as a precondition in the run-up — rather than after it as a consequence. The proposed mechanism: supply is constrained (embargo, revolution, invasion, sanction, blockade) → price spikes → the spike transmits as an inflation and cost shock that squeezes the marginal household and forces central-bank tightening → the tightening and the squeeze, not the oil itself, tip an already-loaded market into decline.

Because it is a sequence claim, it dies cleanly if the record shows supply shocks arriving *after* crashes as often as before them. Sequence is checkable. That is the whole appeal.

## The observer's-note that motivated it (2026, contemporaneous)

Recorded in real time, spring 2026, and kept here as the origin observation — **not** as evidence, and explicitly **not** as an intent claim:
large, well-regarded capital positioned into oil majors (Berkshire's Occidental and Chevron stakes) *ahead of* a period in which supply was progressively removed from the board — Venezuela sanctions/seizure dynamics, then the Iran–US–Israel conflict and the Strait of Hormuz closure that pushed Brent to ~$111 in May 2026. The observation that prompted the column was the *order*: positioning, then supply removal, then price spike, then household squeeze — the crash, if it comes, last. **Per the archive's standing rule, this notes a sequence, never a coordinated intent. The positioning of any investor is public fact; motive is not claimed, tested, or implied.**

## What the record actually says — read this before believing the thesis

The honest result of assembling the dates is that **the sequence holds cleanly in the 1970s and fails in 1990 and 2008.** Two independent, well-sourced analyses reach the same structural conclusion: in 1990 the recession was already dated to the month *before* Iraq invaded Kuwait, and in 2008 oil peaked in July — after the December 2007 recession onset, after Bear Stearns collapsed in March — meaning the downturn was well underway before the oil high. In both cases the oil shock **amplified** a downturn that another force had already started. That is the opposite of a precondition.

So the disciplined statement is narrower and more interesting than the original claim:

> Oil supply shocks are neither necessary nor sufficient for a US market crash. In the 1970s they plausibly **led**; in 1990 and 2008 they **followed or coincided**. The variable that separates "oil shock causes downturn" from "oil shock merely amplifies one" appears to be **pre-existing inflation and leverage conditions**, not the oil event itself — which is exactly what the academic literature (Hamilton; Kilian) concludes: even the 1973–74 price surge was ~75% demand-driven, only ~25% attributable to the exogenous supply cut.

This does not kill the column. It **reshapes** it from "oil squeeze predicts crashes" (false) to a more defensible pair of observations worth keeping:

1. **Oil shocks are a force *multiplier* on an already-loaded structure**, not an independent trigger — which fits the whole thesis of this archive (structure loads first; a spark lands second; the spark is rarely the story).
2. **The pre-shock condition is the real variable.** A supply shock into a low-inflation, low-leverage economy (1990-style, brief) gets absorbed; the same shock into a high-inflation, high-leverage economy (1979-style) helps break it. The oil is the match; the room's gas level decides whether there's a fire.

## Falsification criteria (pre-committed)

- **Kills the precondition version:** already effectively falsified — 1990 and 2008 show shocks following, not leading. The precondition claim should be **retired in public** and replaced with the multiplier claim.
- **Would kill the multiplier version:** a high-inflation, high-leverage economy taking a sustained oil supply shock and *not* seeing amplified market stress — the accelerant failing to accelerate a gas-filled room.
- **Would strengthen it:** the 2026 Hormuz case resolving with a downturn whose severity tracks the pre-shock inflation/leverage readings rather than the oil price itself.
