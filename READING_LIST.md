# Adjacent Literature

These works were identified in May 2025–2026, after this framework was substantially complete. They are listed here as the closest published neighbors to findings that were reached independently from primary sources.

The purpose of this list is not to suggest the framework derives from these works. It is to help readers who want to understand how this project relates to established academic and trade literature — and to document, honestly, what the author had not read when the findings were developed.

---

## On Partisan Economic Outcomes

**Larry Bartels**  
*Unequal Democracy: The Political Economy of the New Gilded Age*  
Princeton University Press, 2008 (expanded edition 2016)

The standard academic reference on differential economic outcomes under Democratic and Republican administrations. Documents that real income growth has been substantially higher under Democratic presidents in the post-WWII period, with the largest differential at the bottom of the income distribution. Closest academic parallel to Finding F-01 (partisan crash asymmetry).

**Alan Blinder and Mark Watson**  
"Presidents and the U.S. Economy: An Econometric Exploration"  
*American Economic Review*, 2016

Quantifies the GDP growth differential at approximately 1.8 percentage points and finds it robust across multiple specifications. Blinder and Watson note the difference is "real" but struggle to fully explain it — much of it appears to be luck (oil shocks, foreign growth) rather than policy. Parallel to F-01 via different methodology.

**Douglas Hibbs**  
"Political Parties and Macroeconomic Policy"  
*American Political Science Review*, 1977

The origin of the "partisan business cycle" research program. Hibbs documented that Democratic administrations prioritize unemployment reduction while Republican administrations prioritize inflation control — producing systematically different macroeconomic outcomes.

## On Financial Crisis History

**Carmen Reinhart and Kenneth Rogoff**  
*This Time Is Different: Eight Centuries of Financial Folly*  
Princeton University Press, 2009

The standard academic reference on debt crises across long history. Documents that sovereign debt crises, banking crises, and inflation crises recur with remarkable regularity across countries and centuries — and that each generation believes its situation is different. Directly relevant to F-04 (inter-crash interval shortening) and F-07 (debt trajectory).

**Charles Kindleberger and Robert Aliber**  
*Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises*  
Originally 1978; multiple updated editions

The canonical work on financial bubbles. Kindleberger documents the recurring anatomy of speculative manias — displacement, credit expansion, euphoria, distress, revulsion — across centuries of financial history. Closest parallel to the asset suppression arc (F-03) in its treatment of recurring institutional behavior.

**Edward Chancellor**  
*Devil Take the Hindmost: A History of Financial Speculation* (1999)  
*The Price of Time: The Real Story of Interest* (2022)

Speculative financial history with attention to institutional dynamics and recurring human behavior. In the lineage of Kindleberger but more accessible. Chancellor writes for the audience most likely to engage with this project's findings.

**Niall Ferguson**  
*The Ascent of Money: A Financial History of the World*  
Penguin Press, 2008

Broader historical narrative on the development of financial instruments and institutions. Useful framing for the asset suppression arc.

## On Cyclical Political Economy

**Arthur Schlesinger Jr.**  
*The Cycles of American History*  
Houghton Mifflin, 1986

Argues for a recurring pendulum between public-purpose and private-interest eras in American political life, with cycles of approximately 30 years. Direct philosophical parallel to the regime-pendulum framing that runs through this project.

**William Strauss and Neil Howe**  
*The Fourth Turning* (1997) and *Generations* (1991)

Develops a generational cycle theory of approximately 80–90 year periods ending in crisis-and-renewal. Influential in finance circles; controversial in academic ones. Relevant to the long-cycle framing.

**Ray Dalio**  
*Principles for Dealing with the Changing World Order* (2021)  
*Big Debt Crises* (2018)

Bridgewater Associates' founder presents a cycle framework directly comparable to this project's working hypothesis. Dalio is in many respects the closest stylistic comparison to this work — empirical pattern recognition applied to long-run historical data, with investment implications drawn explicitly. Published under his own name from a position of substantial credibility.

## On Valuation Cycles

**Robert Shiller**  
*Irrational Exuberance*  
Princeton University Press, 2000 (updated 2015)

Introduces the cyclically adjusted price-to-earnings ratio (CAPE/Shiller P/E), which is the academic standard for cross-era valuation comparison. The P/E figures in this dataset should be standardized to Shiller CAPE for comparability. Data is publicly available from Yale University at monthly resolution back to 1871.

## On Wealth Concentration

**Thomas Piketty**  
*Capital in the Twenty-First Century*  
Harvard University Press, 2014

The standard academic reference on long-run wealth concentration. Provides a factual basis for claims about elite wealth accumulation without requiring unverified figures.

## A Note on Reading Order

If you are reading these works to engage more deeply with this project's findings, the suggested order is:

1. **Bartels** — resolves the partisan asymmetry question in academic context
2. **Reinhart & Rogoff** — grounds the debt trajectory and interval findings
3. **Dalio** (*Changing World Order*) — closest stylistic parallel to the overall framework
4. **Kindleberger** — historical depth on the asset suppression pattern
5. **Shiller** — methodology for valuation comparison
6. **Schlesinger** — the long political cycle framing
7. **Chancellor** (*Price of Time*) — most recent; best on interest rate history
